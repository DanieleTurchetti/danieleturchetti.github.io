Divisors on curves and graphs

Description

Divisors are fundamental objects in algebraic geometry. A divisor on an algebraic curve is a formal linear combination of points with integer coefficients, and can be used to encode information about the regular functions on the curve. If we look at them sufficiently deeply, divisors tell us a compelling story that helps us to classify curves and to understand their arithmetic properties.

In analogy with the case of curves, one can define divisors on finite graphs. These were already known in combinatorics, and have connections with 
game theory (via "chip-firing" on finite graphs) and theoretical physics (via the “abelian sandpile” model).

This correspondence between curves and graphs turns out to be more than just an analogy, and one can actually “send” divisors from curves to graphs, effectively building a bridge from the algebro-geometric to the combinatorial world.

The study of this topic leads naturally to many possible paths: after an initial playful exploration of the different avatars of divisors, students can decide to look more deeply at the algebro geometric aspects (Riemann-Roch theory, Brill-Noether theory, ...), the aspects related to commutative algebra (resolutions of binomial and monomial ideals) or the more combinatorial features (G-parking functions, spanning trees, matrix-tree theorem, ...).
